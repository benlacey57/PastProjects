Why Bude?

England's Finest Walking
on Your Doorstep

Bude occupies one of the most spectacular corners of the entire South West Coast Path. Atlantic cliffs plunge into turquoise water, deep wooded valleys give way to open moorland, and every bend in the trail reveals a view you'll want to pause and savour.

🌊

Coastal Drama

Sheer slate cliffs, surge inlets, natural arches and sea caves line the coast north and south of Bude. Some of England's most arresting coastal scenery is within a half-day's walk.

🌿

Hidden Valleys

Coombe Valley and the Millook Horseshoe are secret green worlds — ancient oak woodland, kingfisher streams and wild garlic carpets — tucked just minutes from the Atlantic clifftops.

🏔️

Moorland & Tors

Dartmoor National Park lies just over an hour away, offering a completely different walking experience — granite tors, wild ponies, blanket bog and enormous skies that feel truly remote.

The Big One

The South West Coast Path

England's longest National Trail stretches 630 miles from Minehead in Somerset all the way around to Poole in Dorset — and the most dramatic section passes directly through Bude. You can step out from the chalet, follow the lane for five minutes, and find yourself on one of the world's great long-distance footpaths.

The North Cornwall section from Bude towards Boscastle and Tintagel is widely considered the path's finest stretch. Knife-edge headlands, waterfalls tumbling straight into the Atlantic, and coastline that changes character with every mile. Dolphins and seals are regularly spotted from the clifftops; in summer, choughs and peregrine falcons nest on the most vertiginous faces.

  • ✓ Starts in Bude town centre — follow the Bude Canal towpath and join the coast path at Compass Point in minutes
  • ✓ Waymarked throughout — the distinctive acorn symbol guides you at every junction, so navigation is straightforward
  • ✓ Walk as little or as much as you like — the path is easily dipped into for short circular walks or ambitious multi-day sections
  • ✓ OS Explorer Map 111 and our host's personal guide are included with every booking

Local Icon

Compass Point
& Efford Beacon

The distinctive octagonal Compass Point Storm Tower stands at the edge of Bude's headland, just a ten-minute walk from the chalet. Built in 1835 to guide ships through the treacherous Bude Bar, this listed building is now one of the town's most beloved landmarks — and the perfect starting point for your first coastal walk.

From Compass Point, head north to Efford Beacon — a low-lying heathland promontory where Cornwall meets Devon. Skylarks sing overhead in spring, and on clear days you can see the whole of Bude Bay laid out below in a sweep of golden sand and white surf. The round trip takes less than two hours and is suitable for most abilities, including families with older children.

Recommended Routes

Walking Trails from the Chalet

Every trail described below starts or can easily be reached from the chalet. Our hosts have personally walked each one and included their favourite stopping points in the included guide.

Challenging 14 miles

Bude to Boscastle

6–8 hrs

The crown jewel of North Cornwall walking. This demanding 14-mile linear route on the South West Coast Path passes through some of the most spectacular coastal scenery in England. Highlights include the Strangles beach (accessible only on foot), Cambeak headland, and the deep inlet at Millook Haven. End with a well-earned cream tea in the witchcraft village of Boscastle.

📍 Starts at Compass Point, Bude · Ends at Boscastle Harbour
🚌 Return by bus (seasonal) or arrange a taxi pickup
⚠️ Rocky, uneven terrain — good boots essential
Easy 4.5 miles

Bude to Widemouth Bay

2–3 hrs

A superb half-day walk ideal for families, dogs and those looking for a gentler introduction to the coast path. Heading south from Bude along the low sea cliffs, the path passes Upton and Wanson Mouth before arriving at the wide sandy beach at Widemouth Bay, where a relaxed seafood café awaits. Return by bus or walk back the same way.

📍 Starts at Summerleaze Beach · Ends Widemouth Bay
🐾 Dog friendly all year · Dogs off lead on the coast path
🍦 Excellent café at Widemouth Bay for lunch
Moderate 7 miles

The Millook Horseshoe

4–5 hrs

One of the best-kept secrets in North Cornwall — a stunning circular walk that descends through ancient Atlantic oak woodland into the Millook Valley, follows a tumbling stream past a working mill and re-emerges on the coast path high above the folded slate cliffs of Millook Haven. The contrast between the green valley and the exposed cliff edge is extraordinary, especially in bluebell season (April–May).

📍 Starts at Millook village · Circular route
🌸 Best April–June for wildflowers and bluebells
🚗 10 min drive from the chalet · Small parking area at start
Moderate–Hard Day trip

Dartmoor Day Walk

Full day

Just 75 minutes by car, Dartmoor National Park offers a completely different walking experience to the coast. The open moorland, granite tors, ancient stone rows and wild ponies create a sense of genuine wilderness. We particularly recommend the Haytor to Hound Tor circular (6 miles) for first-time Dartmoor visitors — stunning views and multiple tors to scramble up, with a proper pub lunch available at the Warren House Inn.

🚗 75 min drive via the A30 — scenic route recommended
🌤️ Weather changes quickly — always carry waterproofs
🍺 Warren House Inn for lunch — one of Dartmoor's finest

Along the Way

Local Landmarks
Worth a Detour

The walking around Bude is rich not just in natural beauty but in stories. Every headland, cove and valley has a history going back centuries — from the wreckers who lured ships onto the Bude Bar to the Victorian engineers who blasted the canal through solid rock. Here are a few of our favourite detours.

🏰

Bude Castle Heritage Centre

Built in 1830 by the eccentric inventor Sir Goldsworthy Gurney, Bude Castle is a wonderfully odd Regency building perched on the cliff overlooking the sea. Now a free museum, it tells the remarkable story of Bude's history as a port town, a seaside resort and a surfing capital. Allow 1–2 hours and combine with a stroll along the canal.

⛪

St Morwenna's Church, Morwenstow

Walk north from Bude along the coast path for 8 miles and you'll reach one of Cornwall's most atmospheric churches, associated with the eccentric Victorian poet-vicar Robert Hawker. His clifftop hut — built from driftwood salvaged from shipwrecks — is one of the smallest properties owned by the National Trust.

🌊

Strangles Beach

Arguably the most dramatic beach in North Cornwall — and reachable only on foot via the coast path. A steep descent through bracken brings you to a wild, dark-sand beach enclosed by towering slate cliffs. Completely inaccessible to anything but feet, it's virtually deserted even in peak season. A place of extraordinary power and beauty.

Before You Set Off

Practical Walking Information

🌦️

Best Time to Visit

The Cornish coast path can be walked year-round, but each season offers something different. Spring (April–June) brings wildflowers, nesting seabirds and quiet paths. Summer (July–August) is warm and sunny but busier. Autumn (September–October) offers the best light, golden bracken and quieter trails — our favourite season. Winter is dramatic and wild.

Busiest: July & August · Best value: April–June, Sept–Oct

🎒

What to Pack

Cornwall's weather is famously changeable. Even on the most glorious summer's day, a sudden Atlantic squall can appear from nowhere — especially on exposed headlands. Always carry:

  • ✓ Waterproof jacket and trousers
  • ✓ Sturdy walking boots (ankle support)
  • ✓ Plenty of water and snacks
  • ✓ Sun cream (the wind is deceptive!)
  • ✓ Fully charged phone with OS Maps app
  • ✓ Cash for cliff-top tea huts
🚑

Safety on the Coast Path

The North Cornwall coast path is spectacular but demands respect. Cliff edges are often unfenced and can be unstable, particularly after wet weather. Children and dogs should be kept under close supervision near the cliff edge at all times.

The 95 bus connects Bude with Bideford and Okehampton. Seasonal coastal services reach Boscastle and Tintagel.

For linear walks, a local taxi can drop you at the start point and collect at the end. We have reliable contacts for you.

Private parking at the chalet. Small car parks at Millook, Morwenstow and other start points — often free or very cheap.
